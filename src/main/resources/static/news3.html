Vertical farming lessons emerge worldwide

A review of 300 global projects shows many vertical farms struggle with scale, cost control, and aligning with consumers—highlighting the challenges. Despite the growing interest in high-tech indoor farming, many vertical farms—especially across North America and Europe—are facing setbacks due to high operational costs and limited market demand. While vertical farming promises efficient, pesticide-free, and climate-resilient food production, the real-world results suggest the need for a more grounded and strategic approach.

A key issue is the cost structure. Building and operating vertical farms involves significant investment in LED lighting, climate control systems, automation, and labor. The expense of producing even simple crops like lettuce and herbs remains much higher compared to traditional field or greenhouse farming. As a result, several startups have struggled to maintain profitability despite achieving technological excellence.

Another common challenge is misalignment with market needs. Many farms have focused more on innovation and scale rather than understanding what consumers want or what local markets can sustain. In contrast, the more successful operations are smaller, leaner, and focused on specialty or high-margin crops, often in regions where climate or land scarcity makes traditional agriculture less feasible.

There are also regional differences. Some Asian countries provide policy support and funding that enable vertical farms to thrive, while others lack the infrastructure or consumer demand. Experts emphasize the importance of choosing the right crops, managing logistics efficiently, and scaling only after market fit is proven.

The overall takeaway is clear: vertical farming is not a one-size-fits-all solution. Instead, it demands smart planning, adaptability, and a focus on long-term economic viability to succeed beyond the initial hype.
